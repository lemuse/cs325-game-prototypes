  • Originality: What is original about your game?

    I began with another program called Twine, which I used to lay out the story states in order, which can be viewed here. It's also a text-based choose-your-own-adventure game.

  • Technical Merit: What is interesting about your game technology?

    It's heavily based in changes of game state. Each choice that the player comes across is given in a new state, which must be called from any other state.

  • Prototype Postmortem: What did you learn from this prototype? What was the easiest or hardest part of making it?

    I learned kind of how game states work. It definitely became a case of reading through and modifying code found via Google, though. The easiest part of making the game was definitely writing the (very simply) story - though adding all of the choices and subsequent states was very, very difficult.

  • High Concept: A one-sentence summary of your game.

    You wake up in the middle of a field with no memory of who you are or how you got there; find the truth about yourself or, possibly, die trying.

  • Theme: State which of the Storymatic themes you used.

    Stargazer, caught, and fainter were all used in different aspects of the story.

  • Mandated Variety: State which input, randomness, genre, and play style you used in this prototype.

    It's a keyboard-input, text-based choose-your-own-adventure game

  • Prototype Goal: What game mechanic is this prototype evaluating?

    Functionality of text-based gameplay: from aesthetics of text scrolling to mapping of gamestates to choices.

  • Player Experience Goals: What experience do you want players to have when playing your game?

    For players who used to have Choose Your Own Adventure books, I want to provide a sense of nostalgia (and the ease of not having to flip pages). For those of us who have not done that, I want to provide an interactive story that is simple enough for anyone to understand and enjoy
